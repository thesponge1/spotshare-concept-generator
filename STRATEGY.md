# SpotShare Website Strategy

## Strategic Conclusion

The SpotShare homepage should NOT be written for a single audience. Visitors arrive from different contexts — some are residents frustrated with guest parking, others are property managers searching for solutions, and some are HOA board members directed here by a colleague. The homepage must explain the shared parking problem clearly and route visitors to the correct audience page.

## The Core Insight

> Buildings often already have parking, but it is trapped inside reserved spots. SpotShare helps make unused parking available in a controlled, building-friendly way.

This is the central idea. Reserved spots sit empty. Guests can't use them. SpotShare connects the two — without permanently changing any spot assignments or building policies.

## Audience Roles

### Residents
- **Experience**: Feel the pain directly when guests can't park
- **Motivation**: Make hosting easier; reduce embarrassment
- **Action**: Advocate to property manager or HOA board
- **Tone**: Empathetic, practical, community-focused

### Property Managers
- **Experience**: Absorb parking complaints, mediate disputes, issue manual passes
- **Motivation**: Eliminate the operational burden of parking
- **Action**: Champion SpotShare internally to the board
- **Tone**: Operational, ROI-focused, implementation-ready

### HOA Boards
- **Experience**: Parking appears on every meeting agenda; budget ownership
- **Motivation**: Close the parking agenda item, earn resident goodwill, reduce liability
- **Action**: Vote to approve SpotShare
- **Tone**: Governance-aware, risk-conscious, ROI-clear

## Homepage Strategy

The homepage serves as a neutral landing page that:
1. Names the problem (unused reserved spots vs. guest parking need)
2. Explains what SpotShare does in plain terms
3. Builds enough trust to earn a click to the right audience page
4. Routes each visitor to their specific page

It should NOT assume the visitor is an HOA board member, a property manager, or a resident. It speaks to all three.

## Storytelling Modes

Each section has 10 variants representing these strategic angles:

| Mode | When to Use |
|------|-------------|
| Problem-First | When the audience has felt the pain and needs validation |
| Outcome-First | When the audience is optimistic and wants to envision the result |
| Question-First | When engagement and recognition are the goal |
| Trust-First | When authority and proof must come before explanation |
| Operational | When the audience thinks in process and efficiency terms |
| Community-Focused | When the building culture and neighbor relationships matter |
| Premium Amenity | When differentiating the building from competitors matters |
| Simple Explanation | When the audience just needs clarity, not persuasion |
| Data-Driven | When the audience responds to metrics and research |
| Emotional Story | When the audience needs to feel the problem before accepting the solution |

## Preset Strategies

### Balanced
Best for: General-purpose marketing, first-time visitors
Mix of problem framing, social proof, and simple explanation. Comfortable for all audiences.

### HOA Buyer Focus
Best for: Targeting board meetings, property management pitches to boards
Heavy emphasis on governance, risk reduction, ROI, and approval process.

### Property Manager Champion
Best for: Reaching operations-focused property managers
Leads with time savings, complaint reduction, and easy implementation.

### Resident Advocacy
Best for: Word-of-mouth, resident referrals, building communities
Emotional, community-centered, focused on the hosting experience.

### Premium Amenity
Best for: Luxury buildings, competitive differentiation, higher-tier HOAs
Positions SpotShare as a modern building amenity alongside smart locks and EV charging.
